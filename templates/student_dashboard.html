BINARY SEQUENCE

1. The Language of Machines

Computers do not understand English or ordinary numbers. At their core, they only understand two states: ON and OFF.

Base-2 Logic

The Binary Number System uses only two digits to represent all data, instructions, and memory inside a computer:

1

ON / TRUE

0

OFF / FALSE

2. Place Values in Binary

Just like decimal has ones, tens, and hundreds places, binary has places that double each time from right to left.

Powers of 2 (2ⁿ)

Let's look at a 4-bit binary sequence that you will find in Level 1 of the Mainframe:

8

2³ (Eights)

4

2² (Fours)

2

2¹ (Twos)

1

2⁰ (Ones)
Decimal Value = Sum of active (1) places

3. Decoding Examples

Example: 1010

Place the binary digits under their values and add the active ones:

0

1

0
8 + 0 + 2 + 0 = 10

Chapter 1: Combustion and Flame

{% elif subject == 'Science' and sub_subject == 'Chemistry' and chapter == '1' and grade|lower == 'g8' %}
COMBUSTION & FLAME

1. What is Combustion?

Combustion is a **chemical process** in which a substance reacts with oxygen and releases heat and light. The substance that burns is called a **fuel**.

2. Necessary Conditions

Three things are required for fire to exist (The Fire Triangle):

FUEL
OXYGEN
HEAT

Removing any one stops combustion!

3. Ignition Temperature

The **lowest temperature** at which a substance starts burning is called its ignition temperature.

  • Paper: Low ignition temp (burns easily)
  • Wood: Higher ignition temp

4. Types of Combustion

Rapid

Burns quickly with heat/light. (e.g., LPG)

Spontaneous

Catches fire on its own. (e.g., Phosphorus)

Explosion

Sudden reaction with sound/gas. (e.g., Crackers)

5 & 6. The Flame

Substances that vaporize on burning produce a flame (Wax, LPG). Charcoal burns without a flame.

Structure of a Candle Flame

Outer (Blue): Hottest, complete combustion.
Middle (Yellow): Moderately hot, partial combustion.
Inner (Dark): Least hot, unburnt vapours.

7, 8 & 9. Fuels & Efficiency

Good Fuel Characteristics:

  • High **Calorific Value** (Energy per kg).
  • Burns cleanly (No smoke/residue).
  • Cheap, safe to store, and transport.

10. Harmful Effects

Burning fuels releases toxic gases like **Carbon Monoxide** (CO), **CO2** (Global warming), and oxides that cause **Acid Rain**.

11-13. Fire Control

Water Extinguisher

For wood/paper. ❌ NEVER for electrical/oil fires.

CO2 Extinguisher

Best for electrical fires. Cuts off oxygen supply.

Mission Briefing Complete! 🛡️

MENSURATION

1. Introduction

Mensuration is the branch of mathematics that deals with the measurement of geometric figures and their parameters like length, area, and volume.

2. Area of Plane Figures

Let's recap the basic formulas for common 2D shapes:

Square

Area = a²

(a = side)

Rectangle

Area = l × b

(l = length, b = breadth)

Triangle

Area = ½bh

(b = base, h = height)

Specialized Polygons

🔷 Parallelogram

A parallelogram is a simple quadrilateral with two pairs of parallel sides.

Area = base × height

(base and height are perpendicular)

📐 Trapezium

A trapezium is a quadrilateral with at least one pair of parallel sides.

Area = ½ × (a + b) × h

(a, b = parallel sides, h = height)

3. Area of a Circle

r
Area = πr²

(r = radius)

Circumference = 2πr

(π ≈ 22/7 or 3.14)

Surface Area & Volume

🧊 Cube

TSA = 6a²
Volume = a³

(a = side)

📦 Cuboid

TSA = 2(lb+bh+hl)
Volume = l×b×h

(l=length, b=breadth, h=height)

🧪 Cylinder

TSA = 2πr(h+r)
Volume = πr²h

(r = radius, h = height)

5. Unit Conversion

  • Length: 1 m = 100 cm
  • Area: 1 m² = 10,000 cm²
  • Volume: 1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³
  • Capacity: 1 litre = 1000 cm³

LIGHT & REFLECTIONS

1. What is Light?

Light is a form of energy that helps us see objects. We can see things only when light falls on them and reflects into our eyes.

Sources of Light

Objects that give out light are called luminous objects.

Sun

Natural Source

Artificial

Bulb, Candle, Torch

Non-luminous Objects

Objects that do not produce light. They are visible because they reflect light from luminous sources.

Examples: Book, table, moon.

2. Transparent, Translucent & Opaque

Objects behave differently when light hits them. Let's see how much light they let through:

Transparent

Allow light to pass through completely.

Glass, Clean Water

Translucent

Allow some light to pass (blurred images).

Butter paper, frosted glass

Opaque

Do not allow light to pass at all.

Wood, Metal, Walls

Scientist Tip!

Opaque objects form the darkest shadows because they completely block the path of light!

3. Formation of Shadows

What is a Shadow?

A dark patch formed when an opaque object blocks the path of light.

Necessary Conditions:

  1. A Source of Light
  2. An Opaque Object
  3. A Screen (surface like a wall or ground)

Features of Shadows

  • Always dark, no matter the object's color.
  • Shows only the shape, not the details (texture, color).
  • Size changes based on the distance from the light source.

4. Pinhole Camera

A simple device that forms images based on the principle that light travels in straight lines.

Image Characteristics:

Real
Inverted (Upside down)
Smaller

5. Reflection & Mirrors

When light hits a smooth surface (like a mirror) and bounces back, it is called reflection.

ECNALUBMA

Lateral Inversion: Ambulance is written in reverse so it reads correctly in rearview mirrors!

Plane Mirror Properties:

  • Virtual: The image cannot be caught on a screen.
  • Erect: The image is upright.
  • Same Size: The image is the same size as the object.
  • Laterally Inverted: Left appears as Right and Right as Left!

Chapter 9: Force and Laws of Motion

1. What is Force?

A push or pull upon an object resulting from the object's interaction with another object.

Balanced vs. Unbalanced Forces ⚖️

Balanced Forces: Net force is zero; object state doesn't change.

Unbalanced Forces: Net force is non-zero; causes acceleration or change in motion.

👉 Only Unbalanced Forces can move a stationary object or stop a moving one!

Chapter 9: Force and Laws of Motion

2. Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia) 🧊

"An object remains in a state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled to change that state by an applied force."

Inertia

The natural tendency of objects to resist a change in their state of rest or of uniform motion.

💡 Crucial Fact: Mass is a measure of inertia. A heavier object has more inertia!

Chapter 9: Force and Laws of Motion

3. Newton's Second Law ⚡

Force = Mass × Acceleration

F = m × a

The second law of motion states that the rate of change of momentum of an object is proportional to the applied unbalanced force.

Momentum (p): The product of mass and velocity. p = m × v

SI Unit of Force: kg m/s² or Newton (N)

Chapter 9: Force and Laws of Motion

4. Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction) 🤝

"To every action, there is always an equal and opposite reaction."

  • Action and reaction forces act on different bodies.
  • They act simultaneously.
  • They are always equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

Using Brackets (BODMAS Rule)

This topic teaches the correct order to solve mathematical expressions.

1. What is BODMAS?

BODMAS tells us exactly which operation to do first.

  • B → Brackets
  • O → Of (means multiplication)
  • D → Division
  • M → Multiplication
  • A → Addition
  • S → Subtraction

We follow this order left to right when needed.

2. Why is BODMAS Important?

Without order, answers can be wrong ❌

Example: 8 + 2 × 5

Wrong way: (8 + 2) × 5 = 50

Correct way: 8 + (2 × 5) = 18

3. Steps to Solve

Step 1️⃣: Solve Brackets first

(6 + 2) × 3 = 8 × 3 = 24

Step 2️⃣: Solve Of (multiplication)

½ of 20 = 10

Step 3️⃣: Division and Multiplication (Left to Right)

20 ÷ 5 × 2
= 4 × 2
= 8

Step 4️⃣: Addition and Subtraction (Left to Right)

10 - 2 + 3
= 8 + 3
= 11

4. Mixed Example

Solve: 12 + 6 × (5 - 2)

  • Step 1: Bracket → (5 - 2) = 3
  • Step 2: Multiply → 6 × 3 = 18
  • Step 3: Add → 12 + 18 = 30

Common Mistakes

  • Ignoring brackets
  • Doing addition before multiplication
  • Not following left-to-right rule

Quick Tricks to Remember

“Brackets first, then multiply/divide, then add/subtract”

Solve from innermost to outermost

[10 - (2 + 3)] = [10 - 5] = 5

Chapter 1: Knowing Our Numbers

📏 Rules of Roman Numerals

Follow these 5 logical rules to construct any Roman numeral perfectly.

Rule 1: Repetition (Adding)

Same symbols can be repeated up to 3 times.
Values are added together.

III = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3

XX = 10 + 10 = 20

Rule 2: No more than 3 repetitions

IIII is wrong for 4.

4 is written correctly as IV.

Rule 3: Smaller before bigger → Subtract

IV = 5 - 1 = 4

IX = 10 - 1 = 9

Rule 4: Smaller after bigger → Add

VI = 5 + 1 = 6

XV = 10 + 5 = 15

Rule 5: Only certain symbols subtract

  • I can subtract from V and X → IV (4), IX (9)
  • X can subtract from L and C → XL (40), XC (90)

Chapter 1: Knowing Our Numbers

🔢 Converting & Common Numbers

Mastering Roman Numeral conversions and common pitfalls.

3. Common Numbers

  • 1 = I
  • 4 = IV
  • 5 = V
  • 9 = IX
  • 10 = X
  • 20 = XX
  • 40 = XL
  • 50 = L
  • 90 = XC
  • 100 = C

4. How to Convert

Roman → Number

Example: XIV = X (10) + IV (4) = 14

Number → Roman

Example: 23 = 20 + 3
= XX + III = XXIII

Common Mistakes

  • Writing IC for 99 (wrong) → Correct: XCIX
  • Repeating V, L, C → These are never repeated

Quick Trick to Remember

“If a smaller number comes before → subtract, after → add”

LIGHT & REFLECTIONS

1. What is Light?

Light is a form of energy that helps us see objects. We can see things only when light falls on them and reflects into our eyes.

Sources of Light

Objects that give out light are called luminous objects.

Sun

Natural Source

Artificial

Bulb, Candle, Torch

Non-luminous Objects

Objects that do not produce light. They are visible because they reflect light from luminous sources.

Examples: Book, table, moon.

2. Transparent, Translucent & Opaque

Objects behave differently when light hits them. Let's see how much light they let through:

Transparent

Allow light to pass through completely.

Glass, Clean Water

Translucent

Allow some light to pass (blurred images).

Butter paper, frosted glass

Opaque

Do not allow light to pass at all.

Wood, Metal, Walls

Scientist Tip!

Opaque objects form the darkest shadows because they completely block the path of light!

3. Formation of Shadows

What is a Shadow?

A dark patch formed when an opaque object blocks the path of light.

Necessary Conditions:

  1. A Source of Light
  2. An Opaque Object
  3. A Screen (surface like a wall or ground)

Features of Shadows

  • Always dark, no matter the object's color.
  • Shows only the shape, not the details (texture, color).
  • Size changes based on the distance from the light source.

4. Pinhole Camera

A simple device that forms images based on the principle that light travels in straight lines.

Image Characteristics:

Real
Inverted (Upside down)
Smaller

5. Reflection & Mirrors

When light hits a smooth surface (like a mirror) and bounces back, it is called reflection.

ECNALUBMA

Lateral Inversion: Ambulance is written in reverse so it reads correctly in rearview mirrors!

Plane Mirror Properties:

  • Virtual: The image cannot be caught on a screen.
  • Erect: The image is upright.
  • Same Size: The image is the same size as the object.
  • Laterally Inverted: Left appears as Right and Right as Left!
